The Sun

After the Moon's distortions, clarity. A child rides a white horse under a blazing sun. Sunflowers grow tall behind a wall. Everything is visible, nothing is hidden, and the child isn't worried about any of it. Fire element. The actual Sun — not a planet, but the star that makes life on this pale blue dot possible. Eight minutes for its light to reach you. Every photon is eight minutes old by the time it touches your face. The Sun card is the simplest card to read and the hardest to live. It says: joy is available. Not later. Not after you fix the thing. Now. The child on the horse isn't celebrating an achievement. The child is celebrating existence. That's the difference between happiness and joy. Happiness is conditional — it depends on things going well. Joy is the recognition that you're a collection of atoms that somehow learned to notice sunlight, and that's enough. Sagan's "pale blue dot" speech is a Sun card moment — look how small we are, and look how miraculous that smallness is. When this card appears, stop complicating it. The answer is simpler than you think. The sun is shining. Go outside.
