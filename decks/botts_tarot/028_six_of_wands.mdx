A rider on horseback. A wreath on the wand. A crowd cheering. You won. The thing you were working toward — it worked. People noticed. The Six of Wands is public recognition, and it's complicated. Not because recognition is bad. Because it changes what you're optimizing for. Before the victory, you were optimizing for the work. After the parade, there's a subtle gravitational pull toward optimizing for the next parade. The crowd isn't cheering for who you are. They're cheering for what you did. Those are different things, and the gap between them is where ego sneaks in. There's always a morning after the victory lap. The trophy goes on the shelf. The crowd moves on to the next spectacle. And you're left asking whether the work was for the thing or for the applause. The Six of Wands doesn't warn you about failure. It warns you about success. Specifically, about letting success become your identity instead of just something that happened to you on the way to more work. When this card appears, enjoy it. Fully. You earned this. Ride the horse. Wear the wreath. Let people cheer. Then get off the horse. Horses are for traveling, not for thrones. The next project doesn't care about your last victory. It only cares whether you show up.
