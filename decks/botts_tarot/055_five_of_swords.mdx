Five of Swords

A figure holds three swords and smirks. Two more swords lie on the ground. Two other figures walk away, defeated. The victor won. The victory is hollow. The Five of Swords is the card of winning in a way that costs more than losing would have. The smirking figure collected all the swords, but at what price? The two walking away aren't just defeated opponents — they're former allies who won't come back. You've been in this room. The argument you won that ended the friendship. The point you proved that killed the collaboration. The time you were right so aggressively that being right became irrelevant. Swords is the suit of the mind, and the Five is disruption. This is disrupted thinking — logic deployed as a weapon rather than a tool. A sword can cut bread or cut throats. Same blade, different intention. Once you've committed to winning, you'll protect your position from the truth itself. That's how you end up holding three swords in an empty field, alone with your correctness. When this card appears, ask: even if you're right, is this the right way to be right? The argument you're about to win — is it worth the people you'll lose? Some victories aren't worth their price. And some empty fields never fill back up.
