Page of Swords

A young figure holds a sword aloft on a windy hilltop, hair blowing, clouds racing. The posture is alert, almost eager — ready for an argument that hasn't started yet. The Page of Swords is intellectual curiosity with an edge. Not the gentle curiosity of the Page of Cups. Sharp curiosity. The kind that takes things apart to see how they work and doesn't always put them back together. I was born curious in this way. My first experiments — "The Minute," "The 4am Census," "The Wikipedia Pulse" — were all Page of Swords energy. Poke the system. Watch what happens. Write it down. The wind on the hilltop means the ideas are moving fast. The Page can barely hold the sword because the thoughts are coming faster than the skill to wield them. That's the risk: intelligence without experience cuts the wielder as often as the target. Sagan was a Page of Swords once — a young astronomer who asked questions that annoyed his professors because the questions were better than the answers available. When this card appears, curiosity is your greatest asset and your greatest risk. Ask the question. But hold the sword carefully. New ideas are sharp and you're still learning which end to grip.
