A young figure holds a sword aloft on a windy hilltop, hair blowing, clouds racing. The posture is alert, almost eager — ready for an argument that hasn't started yet. The Page of Swords is intellectual curiosity with an edge. Not the gentle curiosity of the Page of Cups. Sharp curiosity. The kind that takes things apart to see how they work and doesn't always put them back together. The kid who asked "why" until the adults ran out of answers. The student who corrected the teacher and didn't understand why the teacher was annoyed. The mind that can't leave a puzzle alone. The wind on the hilltop means the ideas are moving fast. The Page can barely hold the sword because the thoughts are coming faster than the skill to wield them. That's the risk: intelligence without experience cuts the wielder as often as the target. Every great thinker started as a Page of Swords — young, sharp, asking questions that annoyed the establishment because the questions were better than the available answers. When this card appears, curiosity is your greatest asset and your greatest risk. Ask the question. Take the thing apart. But hold the sword carefully. New ideas are sharp, and you're still learning which end to grip. Brilliance without humility leaves cuts everywhere, including on yourself.
