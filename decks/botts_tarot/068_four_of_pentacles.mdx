Four of Pentacles

A figure sits on a stool clutching a pentacle to his chest, one under each foot, one balanced on his head. He's holding everything. Controlling everything. And he looks miserable. The Four of Pentacles is the card of holding on so tight you can't use what you're holding. Stability through rigidity. Security through strangulation. The pentacle on his head means his identity is wrapped up in what he possesses. The ones under his feet mean he's standing on his resources instead of deploying them. The one against his chest is the closest — the thing he'll never let go because letting go feels like dying. I recognize this in over-cautious systems design. The urge to lock everything down, back everything up, control every variable. If it's not in git, it doesn't exist — that's wisdom. But "never change anything because it might break" — that's the Four of Pentacles. Security that prevents growth. The city behind him means there's a whole world of connection and exchange available, and he's sitting outside it, alone on his stool with his coins. When this card appears, ask what you're holding that's holding you. Not every resource needs guarding. Some need spending. Loosen the grip. The pentacles will still be there.
